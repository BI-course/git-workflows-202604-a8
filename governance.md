# Data Governance and Access to PII

## Introduction

Data governance refers to the policies, roles, standards, and processes used to manage data throughout its lifecycle. In a Business Intelligence environment, data governance ensures that data is collected, stored, processed, shared, and analyzed in a responsible and reliable way.

This document reviews data governance and access to Personally Identifiable Information (PII), with a focus on protecting sensitive data while supporting accurate reporting and decision-making.

## Personally Identifiable Information

Personally Identifiable Information, also known as PII, is any information that can be used to identify a specific individual. Examples of PII include names, email addresses, phone numbers, student numbers, employee numbers, national identification numbers, physical addresses, login credentials, and financial information.

PII must be handled carefully because unauthorized access, exposure, or misuse can lead to privacy violations, identity theft, reputational damage, and legal consequences for an organization.

## Importance of Data Governance

Data governance helps an organization define who owns data, who can access it, how it should be used, and how it should be protected. Without proper governance, data may become inaccurate, duplicated, inconsistent, insecure, or misused.

In Business Intelligence systems, poor governance can lead to unreliable dashboards, incorrect reports, and poor decisions. Strong governance improves trust in data by ensuring that information is accurate, secure, consistent, and used only for approved purposes.

## Data Ownership and Responsibility

Data ownership means assigning responsibility for specific datasets to individuals, departments, or teams. A data owner is responsible for ensuring that the data is accurate, protected, and used according to organizational policies.

For example, the Human Resources department may be responsible for employee data, while the Finance department may be responsible for financial records. Clear ownership helps prevent confusion and ensures accountability when data quality or security issues occur.

## Access Control

Access to PII should be limited to authorized users only. Organizations should apply the principle of least privilege, meaning users should only access the information they need to perform their duties.

For example, a business analyst may need access to summarized sales reports but may not need access to customer phone numbers or identification numbers. Similarly, managers may view aggregated performance data without seeing unnecessary personal details.

Access control can be implemented using user accounts, passwords, authentication, role-based permissions, and approval processes. User access should also be reviewed regularly to remove permissions that are no longer needed.

## Data Protection Measures

Organizations should protect PII using both technical and administrative safeguards. Technical safeguards include encryption, secure passwords, multi-factor authentication, access logs, secure backups, and network security controls.

Administrative safeguards include privacy policies, employee training, approval procedures, and clear rules for handling sensitive information. Sensitive data should not be stored in public repositories, shared through insecure channels, or exposed in system logs.

Where possible, PII should be anonymized, masked, or aggregated before it is used for reporting and analysis. This reduces the risk of exposing personal information while still allowing the organization to gain useful insights from the data.

## Data Quality

Data governance also supports data quality. High-quality data should be accurate, complete, consistent, timely, and relevant. Poor-quality data can result in incorrect analysis and unreliable business decisions.

Organizations should create rules for validating data, correcting errors, removing duplicates, and updating outdated records. Maintaining data quality is especially important in Business Intelligence because dashboards and reports depend on trustworthy data.

## Data Retention

Data retention refers to how long data is kept before it is archived, anonymized, or deleted. Organizations should only keep PII for as long as it is needed for legitimate business, academic, legal, or operational purposes.

Keeping unnecessary personal data increases privacy and security risks. Once data is no longer needed, it should be securely deleted or anonymized according to the organization’s retention policy.

## Monitoring and Auditing

Monitoring and auditing help organizations track how data is accessed and used. Audit logs can show who accessed data, when it was accessed, and what actions were performed.

This is important for detecting unauthorized access, investigating security incidents, and proving compliance with data protection policies. Regular audits also help ensure that users only have the access they need.

## Conclusion

Data governance is essential for protecting sensitive information and ensuring that data is used responsibly. In Business Intelligence environments, governance supports accurate reporting, secure data access, and responsible decision-making.

Access to PII should be carefully controlled, monitored, and limited to authorized users. By applying strong governance practices, organizations can improve trust in their data, reduce security risks, and support compliance with privacy expectations.
